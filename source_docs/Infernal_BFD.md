# The Infernal BFD

## Bill Chen and Jerrod Ankenman

# 1 System Overview

The Infernal BFD is an amalgam of the Big Forcing Diamond (BFD) system played by Bill Chen, Spencer Sun, Jerrod Ankenman, and others, and the Infernal Machine played by Michael Shuster and Sam Dinkin. The main philosophical idea behind the system is to get in there and compete, to open the bidding very light, to compete very aggressively, and to do so with defined ranges so that we know where we are. Further, we tend not to stretch our agreements in the manner of many players where one or both partners just systematically overbid. Instead, we believe in crafting a system that allows us to bid the way we want to, and then adhering to it.   
   
The main features of the system are:

* A nebulous artificial 1$C$ opening that shows 11-17 and a variety of hands, including 15-17 balanced and many hands with a single minor.  
* A strong, forcing 1$D$ opening.  
* 1 of major openings that are 8-11 and at least four cards and could be canape in the first two seats.  
* 11-14 NT.  
* 2$C$ and 2$D$ show two suited hands of opening strength in the majors and minors, respectively.  
* 2 of a major showing an unbalanced hand 11+-15 with that major.

When the opponents open, we compete aggressively using a combination of Overcall Structure-style power doubles and direct overcalls (very light style, often 4 cards) and an Inverted Raptor structure that shows various two suiters and takeout hands.

## 1.1 Terms

A few terms that are used throughout are defined here rather than in each instance.

**At most semibalanced**: a hand with a singleton, void, or two doubletons with the strength concentrated outside the long suit(s).  For example, AKJxx xx KQJx xx qualifies but Qxxxx AJ KJxx Kx is “balanced.”    
**5+-4+ in the majors/minors**: Unless otherwise specified this means “at least 5 cards in one and 4 cards in the other, could be longer in either or of equal length”.  This applies to other numbers, too, (5+-5+, for example).

## 2.2 We Open (3rd and 4th seats)

In 3rd seat we revert to a more standard approach to opening majors, with weak twos and 1$H$/1$S$ being standard third seat openers with a tendency to open on 4 cards when the suit is appropriate.  
In 4th seat, we increase minimums and point ranges across the board. A guide might be 17 Pearson points instead of the usual 15\. In practice, an auction of three passes to us comes up rarely and when it does 4th seat often has a very good hand.

| Bid | 3rd seat | 4th seat |

# 3 1$C$

The 1$C$ opener is a catchall bid, largely defined by containing all the hands of opening strength that aren’t covered by another opening bid. All hands that open 1$C$ are 11-17 and are usually one of the following hand types:

* 15-17 balanced. We systemically open 1C with 15-17 balanced even with a five card major and typically suppress the 5-card major until we have shown our strength and general shape, bidding it later if we can.  
* 11-17 with a single long minor. Typically this is a six-card or longer minor, the kind of hand that standard 2/1 bidders would open 1$C$/1$D$ and rebid either 2$C$/2$D$ or 3$C$/3$D$. See below for hands with a 5-card minor and a 4-card major. Note that 1$C$ denies 5+-4+ in the minors (open 2$D$ with such hands).  
* 11-17 with at most semibalanced, a 5+ card minor and a 4 card major (or any 4441).  
* 15-17 with at most semibalanced and a 5 card major. We open 1$C$ with these hands in order to take pressure off of the 2M openers. Initially those bids were 11-17 and we found constructive bidding to be challenging with such a wide strength range. 

This bid is completely symmetric with respect to the minors and could be as short as zero clubs.

Most of the responses here are similar to responses to a 1$C$/1$D$ opening in standard. Major suit responses show at least a hand that might be interested in game opposite a maximum, while weaker hands conceal their majors behind a 1$D$ response, which allows opener to describe naturally.

### 3.2.1 1$C$-1$D$

The 1$D$ response is quite nebulous. It essentially contains any hand that doesn’t meet the criteria for another response. The most important of these criteria is the 8+ HCP and a 4 card major, so 1$D$ can contain hands that are quite strong with no major, as well as weaker hands with or without a major. 

The main followups for opener are:

| Rebid | Description |
| :---- | :---- |
| 1$H$/1$S$ | Shows 4+ cards in the suit bid (bid 1$H$ with 4 of both majors) and **at most semibalanced**. WIth balanced hands of 11-14 we would have opened 1NT and with balanced hands rebid 1NT, bypassing majors. Examples:  AKxx xx AQJxx xx,  x Kxxx AKJx KQxx. Note with the special 16-17 and a 5+ card major hand, rebid 2M instead. |
| 1NT | 15-17, balanced. |
| 2$C$/2$D$ | 11-14 and a 6+ card suit. Denies a 4 card major. |
| 2$H$/2$S$ | 16-17, 5+ cards in the major and at most semibalanced. |
| 2NT | 6322 with 15-17 and a 6 card minor. (like a 3m rebid but with a better shot at rightsiding 3NT) |
| 3$C$/3$D$ | 15-16, a good 6+ minor, but not a gambling 3NT-type hand. This bid promises a shortness (else bid 2NT) |
| 3NT | Gambling with a long minor that should run and one outside stopper. |
